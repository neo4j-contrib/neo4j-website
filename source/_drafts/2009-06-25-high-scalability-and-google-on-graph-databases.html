Last month, the Neo4j community buzz was heating up but this month the heat has reached another level. Todd Hoff of High Scalability wrote a short post on Graph Databases and the Future of Large-Scale Knowledge Management, pointing to slides (pdf) by Marko Rodriguez. Hoff says:

Relational databases, document databases, and distributed hash tables get most of the hype these days, but there's another option: graph databases.

Then Hoff delved further into the subject and wrote a piece entitled Neo4j - a Graph Database that Kicks Buttox, which was widely read and and cited with comments like: And donâ€™t just read this piece; bookmark it! Here are some of his points:

Decent documentation, active and responsive email list, a few releases, good buzz. All a good sign for something that has a chance to last a while.

Fully transactional like a real database. Supports JTA/JTS, XA, 2PC, Tx recovery, deadlock detection, etc.

Current implementation is built to handle large graphs that don't fit in memory with durability. It's not a cache, it's a fully persistent transactional store.

The article has a discussion in the comments and also spawned a discussion thread over at MetaFilter under the title Neo4j traverses depths of 1000 levels and beyond at millisecond speed, the latter going more into theoretical questions.

The next thing happening that made graph databases gain increased focus was this post about Pregel, the graph mining infrastructure used internally at Google. It seems Pregel is oriented towards data analysis and not transactional applications.

It's not a coincidence there was a question on Stackoverflow specifically about graph databases and Neo4j!

Julian M Bucknall, CTO of Developer Express wrote a piece on graph databases. He mentions topics like scalability, schema evolution and the sparse table problem of RDBMSs. In the core part of the article he writes:

Finally -- and I'm not trying to nail down the coffin lid here, just pointing out issues with the relational model -- we have what might be called the social media issue or the six degrees of separation problem. More and more of our data forms a network or a graph. ... Persisting this kind of network model is not geared to a relational database ...

The article Should you go Beyond Relational Databases by Martin Kleppmann gives useful hints as to which kind of database could be useful in different applications. Of course he mentions graph databases and Neo4j: he contributed a Scala library for Neo4j, capable of exposing domain specific RESTful JSON APIs. Next he started to collaborate with Andreas Ronge on the Ruby wrapper for Neo4j, which also includes a REST API. The new version including the REST stuff was just released.

Matt Revelle is improving on theClojure support for Neo4j and tweeted:

Just pushed updates to clojure-neo4j. Plenty more to do, feedback welcome. #clojure #neo4j http://github.com/mattrepl/clojure-neo4j/tree/master

If you like your code lispy, the clojure-neo4j library should be well worth a look.
